Customer Empathy Map

Medical/Dermatological Brand — Hybrid Dermocosmetic-Therapeutic Skin Health Brand for Young Peruvian Adults

🧬
Target Segment
Age 18–35 · 65% F / 35% M
NSE B/C+ · Urban Peru
Students & young professionals
Acne-prone & sensitive skin
💭
Think & Feel
▼

They think about their skin constantly — checking mirrors and phone cameras. Frustrated when expensive products fail or cause reactions. Anxious about acne's impact on professional image and social confidence. Overwhelmed by contradictory skincare advice.

Constant skin awareness — checking mirrors, phone front camera, analyzing pores and blemishes multiple times daily
Frustration when premium products (often imported) don't deliver promised results — feels like wasted money
Anxiety that acne/skin problems undermine their professional credibility and social desirability — skin as identity marker
Dream of "effortless" clear skin while knowing intellectually that it requires consistent effort — tension between aspiration and reality
Overwhelmed by contradictory information: dermatologists say one thing, influencers say another, pharmacists recommend based on margins SrcSource: Consumer_Health_in_Peru.pdf — Pharmacists' product recommendations can be driven by commercial incentives; new Law No. 32033 addresses this
👁
See
▼

Social media flooded with skincare routines and trends. Pharmacy shelves split between clinical products and premium imports. Peers with clear skin who seem to have found "the answer." Aruma and Mifarma expanding dermocosmetics sections.

TikTok/Instagram flooded with "skin cycling," "glass skin," and multi-step routine trends — creating aspirational pressure
Pharmacy shelves visually split: clinical-looking pharmaceutical products (Isodine, Dr. Zaidman) vs. premium international dermocosmetics (Eucerin, Bioderma) SrcSource: Dermatologicals_in_Peru.pdf — Table 4: Medifarma leads at 13.2%, Eucerin at 1.5%, Bioderma Nodé at 0.4%
Aruma and Mifarma actively expanding dermocosmetic portfolios with educational campaigns SrcSource: Dermatologicals_in_Peru.pdf — "Key retailers such as Aruma and Mifarma are capitalising on this trend by expanding their dermocosmetic portfolios"
Peers with apparently effortless clear skin who seem to have decoded the "right" routine — triggering comparison and self-doubt
Long dermatologist wait times and expensive private consultations — medical advice is seen as scarce and premium
👂
Hear
▼

Dermatologists recommend prescription products (expensive, clinical). Influencers tout Korean/French brands (aspirational, imported). Friends share mixed experiences. Pharmacists give commercially-influenced recommendations.

Dermatologists: "Use these prescription products" — effective but expensive, clinical, and require ongoing consultations SrcSource: Dermatologicals_in_Peru.pdf — "The trend toward prescription-only dermatologicals... ability of brands to communicate directly with the end consumer is diminished"
Influencers: "This Korean/French brand changed my skin" — aspirational messaging for imported products most can't consistently afford
Friends: Polarized experiences — "I tried everything and nothing works" vs. "This ONE product finally helped" — no consistent signal
Pharmacists: Recommendations often influenced by commercial relationships with manufacturers SrcSource: Consumer_Health_in_Peru.pdf — Law No. 32033 prohibits incentivizing pharmacists to push certain brands over generics
Media: Growing coverage of the Medifarma serum crisis reinforcing consumer concerns about product safety and quality SrcSource: Consumer_Health_in_Peru.pdf — "Medifarma's defective serums caused deaths of several patients... impacted consumer confidence"
🗣
Say & Do
▼

They say they want "simple, effective" routines. They actually buy multiple products across brands, experimenting constantly. They research ingredients online. They sometimes use prescription products without medical supervision.

SAY: "Quiero algo simple que funcione" — verbal commitment to simplicity, but behavior reveals complexity-seeking
DO: Buy multiple products across brands, constantly experimenting — abandon products before giving them time to show results (4–8 weeks minimum)
DO: Research active ingredients online before purchasing — niacinamide, retinol, salicylic acid are recognized terms
CONTRADICTION: Claim to trust dermatologists most, but frequently self-medicate with prescription products bought OTC SrcSource: Consumer_Health_in_Peru.pdf — "Consumers from all income levels self-medicate... some pharmacies sell prescription medicines as OTC products"
DO: Share skincare experiences on social media — both successes (product reviews) and frustrations (rants about failed products)
⚡
Pains
▼

The efficacy-experience trade-off: products that work feel medicinal, products that feel good don't work. Information overload from contradictory sources. Premium import prices. Formulations not optimized for Peruvian climate and skin.

EXTREME: The efficacy-experience trade-off — clinical products that clear acne feel harsh, medicinal, unpleasant; cosmetic products that feel luxurious deliver no therapeutic results SrcSource: Dermatologicals_in_Peru.pdf — "Shoppers are looking for clinical efficacy, but also compatibility with their daily personal care habits"
EXTREME: Information overload and confusion — contradictory advice from dermatologists, influencers, pharmacists, and social media creates decision paralysis
MODERATE: Budget strain — imported dermocosmetics (Eucerin, Bioderma, La Roche-Posay) carry premium prices inaccessible for sustained use
MODERATE: Formulations not optimized for Peruvian conditions — Lima's coastal humidity worsens acne; highland altitude causes dryness; UV exposure varies dramatically by region
MODERATE: Product safety anxiety heightened by the Medifarma serum crisis — consumers now question local product quality control SrcSource: Consumer_Health_in_Peru.pdf — Medifarma crisis "reinforced the importance of traceability, health surveillance, and transparency throughout the value chain"
EMOTIONAL: Shame and social anxiety associated with visible acne — skin problems perceived as failure of self-care rather than a common medical condition
✦
Gains
▼

Clear, healthy skin that gives them confidence. Simple routines that fit their lifestyle. Products that feel luxurious but are scientifically proven. Affordable access to clinical-grade ingredients. Formulations designed for Peru's climate and skin diversity.

Required: Visible skin improvement within 4–8 weeks — without visible results, all other benefits are irrelevant
Expected: Pleasant sensorial experience — lightweight textures, non-greasy absorption, subtle natural scent — making the routine enjoyable, not obligatory SrcSource: Dermatologicals_in_Peru.pdf — "opportunities for hybrid formats and a more versatile positioning" emerging from beauty-health convergence
Desired: Simple 2–3 step routine with clear instructions — eliminates the complexity and decision fatigue of multi-product regimens
Desired: Understanding what's in their products and why each ingredient works — ingredient transparency builds trust and engagement
Unexpected: Products validated by Peruvian dermatologists and formulated for local climate zones — feeling that someone designed this specifically for them
Unexpected: Affordable access to clinically-effective ingredients (niacinamide, salicylic acid, azelaic acid) without premium import pricing — 30–40% below international brands SrcSource: Dermatologicals_in_Peru.pdf — Eucerin at only 1.5% market share suggests premium pricing limits mass adoption; opportunity for mid-priced entrant
★ Core Customer Insight
"Despite following complex skincare routines and researching ingredients obsessively, young Peruvian consumers actually feel less in control of their skin, because the market forces them to choose between products that work (clinical dermatologicals) and products they want to use (beautiful, sensorial cosmetics) — when what they really need is both in one."
Data Justification: Dermocosmetics are gaining market share from traditional OTC dermatologicals precisely because they offer the aspirational experience consumers crave, while the broader beauty-health convergence shows consumers integrating therapeutic products into daily routines. Retailers like Aruma and Mifarma are accelerating this shift. The gap isn't efficacy alone or aesthetics alone — it's the forced separation of both. The consumer needs a product that resolves this tension without compromise.
